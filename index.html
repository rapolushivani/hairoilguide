The Craft

How to Make It —
The Traditional Process

In Ayurveda, the oil-making process (called Taila Paka Vidhi) is considered sacred. The slow heat method extracts the deepest bioactive compounds from each herb into the oil. This is not cooking — it is a transferring of intelligence from herb to oil. Patience is the most important ingredient.

Step 01 — Day Before
Prepare the Chebe Infusion
In a small glass jar, combine 1 tsp chebe powder with the 15ml of jojoba oil. Seal and leave overnight (8–12 hours). Chebe's active compounds — resin-based — are fat-soluble and require time to fully transfer. The next morning, strain through fine muslin cloth. This is your chebe-infused jojoba. Set aside.
🌙 Tradition: The Bassari women of Chad would mix chebe with the fat of locally sourced seeds and apply as a paste. We bring the same intelligence into a oil format.
Step 02 — Morning
Set Up Your Double Boiler (Bain-Marie)
Fill a saucepan with 2–3 inches of water. Place a stainless steel or glass bowl on top — this is your Ayurvedic taila vessel. The bowl must not touch the water. Bring water to a gentle simmer only — never a rolling boil. High heat destroys phytonutrients. The entire process must stay below 65°C (150°F).
⚠️ Critical: Use a kitchen thermometer. Overheating is the most common mistake — it converts beneficial compounds into inert compounds and can create rancidity.
Step 03
Combine Base Oils & Add Herb Powders
Add all base oils to the bowl: coconut, sesame, castor, argan, and pumpkin seed (hold back jojoba for later — it goes in after with the chebe). Gently warm to 50–55°C. Then add all herb powders one at a time — bhringraj, amla, brahmi, hibiscus, fenugreek, neem. Stir gently with a wooden spoon (metal reacts with compounds). The oil will take on a beautiful deep green-brown colour.
🌿 You will smell the herbs releasing their essence into the oil. This is exactly as it should be. The Ayurvedic texts describe this as the herbs "entering" the oil.
Step 04 — 1.5 to 2 Hours
The Slow Infusion
Maintain gentle heat at 50–60°C for 1.5 to 2 hours. Stir every 20–30 minutes. You are watching for the classic Ayurvedic test: when you take a small pinch of the herb sediment and roll it between your fingers, it should form a wick — no moisture, no crumbling. This means all water content has evaporated and the oil is properly prepared. Do not rush this.
📖 Classical reference: Ashtanga Hridayam describes the Mridu Pak (soft stage) — when the sediment rolls into a wick without sticking, the oil is ready. This is the exact same test used in 600 CE.
Step 05
Strain & Cool
Remove from heat. Allow to cool to 40°C. Strain through a fine muslin cloth or cheesecloth (doubled) into a clean glass bowl, squeezing the herb mass firmly to extract every drop of infused oil. Discard the spent herb mass. Your base oil is now a living, herb-rich infusion. Allow to cool fully to room temperature (25°C or lower).
💡 Never add essential oils to hot oil — their volatile therapeutic compounds evaporate at temperatures above 35°C. Room temperature is essential for this next step.
Step 06
Add Essential Oils, Black Seed & Moringa
Once oil is fully at room temperature, add: the chebe-infused jojoba (15ml), black seed oil (5ml), moringa oil (3ml), and Vitamin E (½ tsp). Then add essential oils drop by drop: rosemary (25 drops), peppermint (15), lavender (12), cedarwood (10), thyme (5). Stir gently but thoroughly for 2 minutes.
🌹 The essential oil blend mirrors the famous Hay et al. (1998) alopecia areata study formulation — rosemary, lavender, cedarwood, thyme in jojoba/grapeseed — which was included in British dermatology guidelines.
Step 07
Bottle, Label & Cure
Pour into a dark amber glass bottle with a dropper top. Label with the date. Leave undisturbed for 24–48 hours before first use — this "curing" period allows the compounds to fully bind and harmonise. Store in a cool, dark cupboard. Avoid plastic containers — oil pulls compounds from plastic over time.
🏺 Use within 12–18 months. The Vitamin E will extend shelf life. If the oil ever smells rancid (sour, paint-like), it has oxidised — discard and make fresh.
The Practice

The Application Ritual

Every great hair tradition — from the Ayurvedic abhyanga (oil massage) to the West African pre-wash soak to the Middle Eastern weekly hammam treatment — agrees on one thing: how you apply the oil is as important as what is in it. The scalp massage is not optional. It is half the treatment.

🌡️
Warm the Oil
Place your bottle in a bowl of warm water for 5–10 minutes. Warm oil penetrates the scalp and shaft significantly better than cold oil. Never microwave. Temperature: body warm, not hot — around 37–40°C. This mimics the Ayurvedic practice of using warm taila for abhyanga.
🖐️
Section & Apply to Scalp
Part your hair into 4–6 sections. Apply oil directly to the scalp along each part — not to the hair first. Use a dropper or your fingertips. Focus on areas of concern: temples, crown, nape. The scalp is where growth happens. 5–8 drops per section is sufficient.
💆
The Scalp Massage — 10 Minutes
Use the pads of your fingers (not nails). Circular motions, medium pressure. Move from hairline inward. Then from nape upward. Research shows scalp massage alone (4 min/day) increases hair thickness after 24 weeks by stretching dermal papilla cells. Combined with this oil, the effect compounds. This is non-negotiable.
🕐
Leave Time
Minimum: 2 hours. Ideal: overnight (cover with a silk or satin bonnet — never cotton, which pulls and absorbs). The 8-hour overnight window allows every compound to reach maximum penetration. West African and Indian grandmothers have always known this — overnight oiling is universal across traditions.
🚿
Wash Out Properly
Apply a sulphate-free shampoo to dry hair first, work it into the scalp before adding water. This emulsifies the oil and lifts it without stripping the scalp. Then add water and lather, rinse, and repeat if needed. Never apply water first — it locks the oil in and makes it harder to remove.
📅
Frequency
2–3 times per week for the first 3 months (the intensive phase). Then 1–2 times per week for maintenance. Consistency over 90+ days is what creates real results. Every tradition in the world agrees: this is a practice, not a one-time treatment. Commit to it as you would to any habit that changes your body.
🧘
The Mental Layer
Ayurveda teaches that the scalp massage is also a meditation. Stress (cortisol elevation) is directly linked to hair loss — it pushes follicles into the telogen (shedding) phase. The lavender and brahmi in this oil work biochemically on cortisol. But the ritual itself — 10 minutes of quiet, focused self-care — is its own powerful medicine.
🌙
The New Moon Practice
Many indigenous traditions (Indian, West African, Mesoamerican) tie hair care to the lunar cycle — oiling deeply during the new moon, when the body is in a receptive, inward state. Whether you follow this or not, the deeper wisdom is about rhythm and regularity. Your hair responds to consistent attention over time, not sporadic intensity.

Wisdom & Questions

Tips, Cautions & FAQs

01
Always patch test first
Apply a small amount to the inner wrist 24 hours before use. Essential oils — especially thyme and peppermint — can cause irritation in sensitive skin. Dilution ratios in this formula are safe for most people, but individual sensitivity varies.
02
Expect 8–12 weeks minimum
Hair growth cycles are 3–6 months long. You cannot change the anagen phase in 2 weeks. Most traditions speak of 3–6 month commitments. Clinical trials measured outcomes at 3, 6, and 12 months. Consistency and patience are the active ingredients most people skip.
03
Less is more on the hair shaft
This oil is designed primarily for scalp application. A few drops run through the mid-lengths and ends for moisture sealing is fine, but over-application to the hair shaft without washing will cause buildup. The scalp is the target, not the strand.
04
Pregnancy caution
Thyme, peppermint, and rosemary essential oils should be used with caution during pregnancy. Consult your doctor before using essential oil-containing products. You can make a gentler version using only the base oils and herbal infusion without essential oils.
05
Source quality ingredients
Cold-pressed, unrefined, organic — these are not marketing words here, they are functional requirements. Refined oils have had their bioactive compounds stripped away. Pesticide-laden herbs introduce toxins to your scalp. The formula is only as good as what goes in it.
06
Hair loss has many causes
This formula addresses topical/nutritive and DHT-related hair loss most effectively. If your hair loss is sudden, patchy, or associated with health changes, see a dermatologist or trichologist. Thyroid disorders, nutritional deficiencies, and autoimmune conditions require medical attention, not oil alone.
07
Scalp massage amplifies everything
A 2019 study showed that scalp massage alone (without any oil) increased hair thickness after 24 weeks. The mechanical stimulation stretches dermal papilla cells and upregulates growth genes. Combined with this oil, the absorption and activation is compounded. Never skip the massage.
08
Store in amber glass, never plastic
Plant oils are sensitive to light and heat. UV light catalyses oxidation. Plastic can leach compounds into the oil over time. An amber or cobalt glass bottle with a dropper top is the ideal container. Keep it in a cool, dark drawer — not the bathroom shelf which gets warm and humid.
09
Simplify if needed
Can't source all ingredients? The most impactful core is: coconut oil + sesame oil + bhringraj powder + amla powder + rosemary essential oil + peppermint essential oil. That five-ingredient version captures the heart of this formula and is backed by the strongest evidence. Start there and build.
10
Support from within
No topical oil compensates for severe nutritional deficiency. Hair is made of protein — ensure adequate protein intake. Iron, zinc, biotin, and B12 deficiencies are among the most common causes of hair loss. The oil works on the outside. Nourish the inside too: whole foods, adequate sleep, and stress management complete the picture.
